# What Is Self-Hosting? (And Why Contractors Are Switching to It)

You've probably heard the term "self-hosting" thrown around lately. It sounds technical. It sounds like something for IT guys in server rooms, not plumbers and electricians with crews to manage.

But it's actually a pretty simple idea — and it's saving small trade businesses hundreds of dollars a month.

Here's the plain-English version of what it is, why it matters, and whether it's right for you.

---

## The Normal Way: You Rent Software

Right now, the way most field service software works is this: a company builds an app, hosts it on their servers, and charges you every month to use it.

You log in, you use it, you pay the bill. When you stop paying, you lose access. If the company raises prices, you either pay up or find something else. If they shut down, your data might disappear.

You never own anything. You're a tenant.

---

## The Self-Hosted Way: You Own the Software

Self-hosting means the software runs on *your* hardware, in *your* building (or a small server you rent for $10–15/month). You're not logging into someone else's platform. You're running your own.

Think of it like the difference between renting an apartment and owning a house. You're responsible for it, but it's yours. Nobody can raise your rent. Nobody can kick you out. All your stuff stays where you put it.

For a trade business, that looks like:

- A small computer (about the size of a thick book) sitting in your office
- It runs your CRM, your scheduling, your invoicing, your crew communication — everything
- You access it from your phone or laptop just like any other software
- Your data never leaves your hands

---

## What Does It Actually Cost?

This is where it gets interesting.

Most self-hosted tools used by trade businesses are **open source** — which means the software itself is free. You're not paying a software company for a license. You're just running it yourself.

The only real ongoing cost is a small server to handle internet traffic — usually $5–15/month. Plus the one-time cost of the mini PC hardware, typically $400–600.

Compare that to $200–500/month for traditional field service platforms. You break even in a few months, and after that you're keeping that money.

---

## "Open Source" — What Does That Mean?

Open source just means the code behind the software is publicly available. Anyone can look at it, use it, and improve it. It's the opposite of "proprietary" software, which is locked up and controlled by one company.

Some of the most reliable software in the world is open source — including tools used by banks, hospitals, and governments. It's not amateur hour software. It's often *more* trustworthy than paid alternatives because thousands of people can inspect and verify it.

For a contractor, what matters is this: there's no company that can suddenly jack up your price, change the terms, or shut down and take your data with them.
